**DOI**: [10.5465/amj.2024.0199](https://doi.org/10.5465/amj.2024.0199)
**Published**: 2026-01-30

**Fields & Theories**:

- **Fields**: Strategic Management, Social Networks, Organizational Theory, Gender Studies
- **Theories**: Social Network Theory, Gender Homophily, Network Resilience, Resource Dependency Theory

**Abstract**:

> Social networks play a role in exacerbating or ameliorating inequality. The persistence of network-based inequality is well documented. However, the mechanisms that disrupt this unequal access to resources remain poorly understood. This study investigates how structurally disruptive events, such as mergers and acquisitions (M&As), reshape gender homophily and resource sharing in networks. We examine how 59 M&As shape the evolution of 82,064 physicians’ referral and patient-sharing networks. We find that disruptive events enhance women’s access to resources by altering gender-homophily dynamics in formation, and maintenance. By demonstrating how gendered network dynamics evolve in response to disruptions, this study contributes to scholarship on gender inequality, network resilience, and conditions under which homophilous ties can mitigate disparities.

- **Fields**: Organizational Behavior, Human Resource Management, Diversity and Inclusion, Disability Studies
- **Theories**: Ideal Worker Norm, Stigma Theory, Identity Work, Ableism

**Abstract**:

> Implicit in the ideal worker norm is the assumption of the ideal worker body, a body that is always ready and willing to meet work demands. Yet, bodies vary in their ability to meet this ideal. ### 5. Media Review: Technology, Temporality, and Organization in Unrest

**Authors**: Moritz J. Kleinaltenkamp

**DOI**: [10.1177/01708406261418186](https://doi.org/10.1177/01708406261418186)
**Published**: 2026-01-22

**Fields & Theories**:

- **Fields**: Organizational Theory, Management History, Science and Technology Studies
- **Theories**: Temporality, Sociomateriality, Technological Transformation

**Abstract**:

> Unrest (original Swiss title: Unrueh) by Cyril Schäublin is a film about workers, activists, and entrepreneurs in a Swiss watchmaking town at the end of the 19th century. Though fictional and depicting a period of profound technological transformation, Unrest eschews a dramatized plot and thus often feels like a documentary, anachronistically shot in high-definition video at a time when photography was in its infancy. This observational style, paired with the film’s thematic focus, makes Unrest a fertile ground for organizational theorists. The film examines how the commodification and spread of technologies reshape temporal landscapes (Tavory & Eliasoph, 2013)—the cultural narratives about time that influence actors’ experiences and agency. Viewed through this lens, Unrest raises important questions about the interaction of technology, temporality, and organizing. After providing a summary of the film, this review highlights two key insights that Unrest offers to organizational theory. These relate to (1) the naturalization of clock time and (2) the collapsing of temporal and spatial experience. I conclude by discussing the opportunities these insights provide for future research. Set in 1877 in the town of St. Imier, Unrest follows Josephine, Pyotr, and Roulet. Josephine works assembling the “unrest wheel,” a delicate watch mechanism, in Roulet’s watchmaking factory. Roulet, a capitalist, oversees operations run by men in scientific garb who measure workers’ efficiency with pocket watches in a precursor to Taylorism. These managers enforce high quotas, reporting unproductive workers to the police.
